# The two workflows

Both start from the same geometry. They differ only in what you print.

![The two workflows](media/workflows.svg)

---

## B — Print the shells (default)

**You print trays. You pour plaster into them.**

The part appears as a raised core on each tray's floor, and plaster sets around it.
Print once, cast as many plaster molds as you like — which is the whole point if you
are building a rack of molds to rotate through a casting cycle.

### The idea that makes it work

Both halves are poured with their **parting face down**, against the flat floor of
the tray.

That face is the one that has to be dead flat and precisely keyed. Forming it against
printed plastic gets you that for free: no bedding a model in clay, no screeding the
critical face, no carving natches with a coin.

It also means the **lower half must be mirrored** before its tray is cut, because in
the mold's own frame its parting face points *up*. Miss that and its registration
cones stick out of the pour opening and get screeded flat off — leaving the halves
nothing to locate on.

### At the bench

1. Print both trays.
2. Soap or spray the inside. The draft on the walls does most of the work; release
   agent makes it effortless.
3. Mix plaster to the weights the app gives you. **Weigh both the plaster and the
   water.**
4. Pour until proud of the rim.
5. **Tap the tray on the bench for a full minute.** Air bubbles rising against a core
   become pits on the face of every pot you ever cast from that mold.
6. Screed level with a straightedge. That face becomes the back of the mold half, and
   the mold will stand on it.
7. Wait until set *and cool* — plaster gives off heat as it goes off, and warm plaster
   is not done.
8. Flex the tray away. The parting face comes out flat and already keyed.

The half with cones and the half with sockets mate one way only.

## A — Print the positive

**You print the part. You pour plaster around it.**

This is the workflow a potter already knows, with the worst step removed.

Normally you bed the model in clay up to the parting line by hand: slow, messy, and
only as accurate as your thumbs — and every error shows up as a misaligned seam on
every pot the mold ever makes.

The **printed bed plate** replaces it. It has the part's exact silhouette cut through
it, so the model can only sit at the parting plane, at exactly the right height, every
time. The registration cones are printed onto it, so the natches are placed by the
same geometry that placed the parting line.

### At the bench

1. Print the positive, the bed plate, and the cottle.
2. Drop the positive through the hole in the bed plate.
3. Assemble the cottle around it. Seal the joints with clay so plaster cannot weep out.
4. **Soap every surface plaster will touch.** Skip this and the plaster keys into the
   print, and neither survives separation.
5. Pour the upper half. Tap the walls to bring bubbles up.
6. Wait until hard and no longer warm — about 45 minutes.
7. Flip the assembly and remove the bed plate. The cones it leaves behind are your keys.
8. **Soap the exposed plaster parting face, thoroughly.** This is the step people skip,
   and the two halves then fuse into one useless block.
9. Pour the second half. Wait, separate, pull the print out.

## Which one?

| | Shells (B) | Positive (A) |
|---|---|---|
| You pour plaster… | **into** printed trays | **around** a printed part |
| Reusable for many molds | Yes — print once, cast repeatedly | No — the cottle is reusable, the setup is not |
| Hand skill required | Almost none | Soaping, flipping, judging the set |
| Plastic used | More | Less |
| Best for | Batching molds for production | One-offs; the traditional route |

If the goal is a rack of molds cycling through a casting rotation, it is B.
